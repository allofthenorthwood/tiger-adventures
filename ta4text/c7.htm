"I know her," said the cab driver.

Tsering's shop was a dark hole in a wall on a quiet street. "Good afternoon," she said. "Can I help you?"

Maybe my adventure with Sanjoy should have taught me better, but I decided to continue my research in the same spirit of subterfuge. "Yes," I said. "I've got some tiger bones to sell."

Tsering looked suspicious. "Tiger bones?" She frowned. Perhaps it was uncommon for someone like me to be involved in the tiger trade. Oh well--it was too late to change my story now. "How many?" she asked.

"A boxful," I said vaguely. "They're at my hotel."

Tsering nodded slowly. "I can offer you an excellent deal. I will trade you one pound of shahtoosh wool for three and a half pounds of tiger bone."

"Shahtoosh?" I asked. "What's that?"

She looked suprised. "Shahtoosh is the king of wool," she explained. "It's made from our very own Tibetan Antelope, the Chiru, a very rare animal even in Tibet. With this wool, you can make a wool shawl that will sell for more than two thousand dollars in Europe."

I was stunned. Simple greed was driving both the tiger and the chiru toward extinction.

But why were shahtoosh shawls so valuable? "Why are they worth so much?" I asked.
