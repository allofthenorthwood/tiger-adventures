"Dangerous work?" said Ranjit. "Perhaps for a wealthy person like you. But tigers are not the romantic creatures that you foreigners think they are. They attack and kill our cattle, our goats, even our children sometimes. Gopal must protect his family from these predators. And he must earn money to support his family."

"I suppose so," I said. "Is this his only source of income?"

Ranjit turned to Gopal and asked him the same question in Hindi. Gopal said, "Not with what you pay me. How many tigers do I bring you in a year? Five, ten? And still my family does not have enough food to eat. What are you charging the foreigner for these tiger skins? Five hundred dollars? Seven hundred? And yet you pay me only 1,000 rupees--not even $30--for it. You take what is mine. You're no better than the government bureaucrats. They took our land and gave it to the tigers. They promised to help us find other ways to support ourselves and our families. But they have done nothing."

Ranjit cut him off. "You don't know your good luck," he told Gopal, still speaking in Hindi. "We'll sell the tiger skins now, and still have the bones to sell another day."

Then Ranjit turned to me and smiled. In English, he said, "Gopal grows a few crops, but he's much happier helping me bring tiger skins to you. If you wish, we can bring you more tomorrow. There are many more tigers in the forest. The government says there are over 100 tigers in the Royal Bengal Forest Reserve alone."

I could not hold my tongue. "Some conservationists say there are only a few dozen left. It wouldn't take many poachers to wipe out the tiger population here."

Ranjit squinted suspiciously at me. "Why do you care what conservationists say?"

I shrugged, suddenly worried. I could see that Sanjoy was worried now too. Hastily, I explained...

"I don't want my supply of tiger skins to dry up."

"Aren't you worried the government will really crack down on poaching?"
