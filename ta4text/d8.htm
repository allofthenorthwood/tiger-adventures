On my return home, I called Dr. Tiger and asked, "How can the tiger trade be stopped?"

He said, "Banning the use of tiger parts in medicine is a good start. Unfortunately, it doesn't solve the problem. Such bans have already been imposed in almost all Asian countries, but they have not stopped the trade.

"There are new synthetic alternatives to tiger medicines," he added. "We hope that people will switch to these once they understand that, chemically, the synthetic medicines are identical to what they've been using for centuries."

"Is that all?" I asked.

"I've heard about other schemes. Some people have proposed breeding tigers on farms specifically to harvest their bones and other parts for medicines. These 'tiger farms,' however, have proved unprofitable. It's much cheaper to buy parts of a poached tiger than raise a tiger from birth. Another suggestion has been to develop a tiger hunting program, but tiger populations are much too endangered to risk such a thing."

"So what can we do?" I asked him.

"Write your article," he said. "See if your school newspaper or the newsletter at your job or church will print it."

"Learn more about the threats facing tigers, then tell people about them. The more people who know about the tiger's dire predicament, the more luck we'll have working to save the tiger."

"You can also join Bittu Sahgal's Cub and Sanctuary Magazine, and read the letters he's written to kids about tigers in India

"And learn about ways that kids are helping to save tigers. Write a letter, collect signatures on a petition, have a bake sale...there are lots of things you can do!

"Okay," I said. "What else?"

"Some people in Asia are doing good work to stop poaching. Support them.

"Or write President Bill Clinton and Vice-President Al Gore."

Dr. Tiger had given me a lot of suggestions about what I could do to help stop poaching. It sounded like a lot of work--but it would be worth it. Time to get to work.
