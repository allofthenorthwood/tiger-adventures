Ranjit turned to Gopal and, speaking in Hindi, asked him: "How many tigers have you taken this year?"

Gopal replied in Hindi. "I don't know. Five, ten, perhaps. Not enough. My family barely has enough food to eat. If you would pay me more, then it would be better."

Ranjit snapped, "Don't get greedy!"

Anger flashed across Gopal's face. "What are you charging the foreigner for these tiger skins? Five hundred dollars? Seven hundred? And yet you pay me only 1,000 rupees--$30--for it. You are hardly better than the government, which takes my land and gives it to the tiger, then forgets its promise to help me find other ways to support my family."

Ranjit cut him off. "You don't know your good luck," he told Gopal, still speaking in Hindi. "We'll sell the tiger skins now, and still have the bones to sell another day."

Then Ranjit turned to me and smiled. In English, he said, "Gopal says he has killed only a few tigers. There are many more tigers in the forest. The government says there are over 100 tigers in the Royal Bengal Forest Reserve alone."

I could not hold my tongue. "But I've heard that there are only a few dozen left. It wouldn't take many poachers to wipe out the tiger population here."

Ranjit squinted suspiciously at me. "Who told you that?"

I shrugged, suddenly worried. I didn't want to admit that I knew any tiger conservationists. And I could see that Sanjoy was worried now too. Hastily, I explained...

"I don't want my supply of tiger skins to dry up."

"Aren't you worried the government will really crack down on poaching?"
