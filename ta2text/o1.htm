Zoo Unveils New Tiger Exhibit

Opening day a success despite complaints

Wilson Hopes to Breed Tigers

Donald T. Wilson, owner of Don Wilson's Wildlife Wonderland, expressed hope yesterday that his captive tiger and the tiger at the zoo would be bred. "It's a win-win situation," he said. "They've got a male tiger, I've got a female tiger. Why not let nature take her course?"
Wilson, who fought zoo officials for custody of the tiger, says he feels no ill will against the zoo. "Of course they wanted the tiger--they've got to attract tourists to the zoo. I just wish they were more devoted to educating our young people about wildlife. But by breeding our tigers, I think we can make a positive step forward together. I sincerely hope it comes to pass."

The San Gabriel Zoo unveiled its new exhibit yesterday to house the Sumatran tiger which briefly roamed the area earlier this year.
According to Zoo officials, the tiger enclosure incorporates the best and most current thinking in zoo exhibit design. "We've provided the tiger with a natural environment and many things to enrich its life, such as scratching logs, water, and heat pads, and of course a healthy diet. I hope everyone comes out to the zoo to get to know our newest resident."
However, some visitors to the zoo yesterday said the tiger did not appear as happy as they would have liked. "I saw him throw up!" said Danny Bence, age 10. "Right in front of us!" Zoo officials denied that the tiger was not in the best of health, but Victor Sanchez, a botanist at El Paso Zoo who came to San Gabriel for the occasion, was concerned about the vegetation. "From what I could see, they planted poison sumac right in the tiger's enclosure. That's toxic to tigers. It won't kill him,

An employee of the San Gabriel Zoo who asked to remain anonymous also voiced concern about the tiger's condition. "They feed him whenever he wants to eat. For a tiger, that's all the time. He's looking chubby already, and it's only going to get worse." Dr. Armitage Douglas, a veterinarian for the American Association of Zoos and Aquariums, said there was a some basis for this concern. "In the wild, the scarcity of game regulates a tiger's eating schedule. In a zoo, the keeper has to regulate that schedule. A tiger's instincts tell him to eat whenever he can--so of course a tiger which is fed on demand will gain weight."
Dr. Douglas added another cause for concern about the tiger's care. "If you let him eat in the exhibit, you can't monitor his diet. The first thing a tiger does when it's sick is stop eating, but you wouldn't notice if you didn't bring it in every night to feed it. This tiger could become very sick before anyone even noticed."
