The Tiger Information Center

Worksheet for Save the Tiger! Adventure
on the Tiger Information Center's web site (www.5tigers.org)

1. Who are you? What's your job? Zookeeper at the San Gabriel Zoo in New Mexico.

2. Why does Don Wilson say the Zoo's tiger should be bred with his female? What are his arguments? Wilson does not agree with the tiger conservation and breeding policies set by the American Zoological Association (AZA) and followed the the City Zoo. He accuses the Zoo of wanting "to create an elite breed of purebred tigers. Ordinary, working-class tigers like our Toni just don't cut it!"

He also points out that the City Zoo may not breed the male tiger for years, due to limits set by the AZA on the number of captive tigers in North American zoos. Wilson says he has plenty of space for cubs, and "If we get too many, we'll just ship some back to Asia and release them back into the wild."

3. What is your response? Students can go Wilson's route (and lose their job), or they can make this argument: " The purpose of the Tiger Species Survival Plan (SSP) is not simply to breed as many tigers as we can. If we did that, we'd soon run out of room--and money to care for the tigers. We all want to save the species--that's a primary mission for zoos today. But we have to do so in a scientific manner. We don't even know what subspecies of tiger your female (Toni) is. If we bred our tiger with yours, we could end up with hybrids across subspecies. No one knows what kind of hybrid the cubs would be--that's the point, and the danger. There's nothing wrong with a hybrid animal itself, but hybrid animals hurt conservation efforts by muddying the genetic pool. The only way to preserve the genetic strength and diversity of captive tigers is through scientific methods.

4. Summarize your talk to the picketers outside your office: Students can summarize this speech: "Don Wilson may have found that his tiger is Sumatran, just as ours is, but that doesn't mean we can breed them. The purpose of zoos' captive management and breeding programs is to build a "genetic insurance policy" for the future. Someday the wild tiger population may become so small and inbred that it cannot survive without help. We may have to strengthen it by reintroducing new genes into the wild population--either the genetic code alone or the animals themselves.

"So the point today is not to breed as many tigers as we can, but to maintain as much genetic diversity as possible among captive tigers. We may know that Toni the Tiger is Sumatran, but we don't know who her parents , much less her grandparents, were. Without knowing her lineage, and her genetic history, we don't know how she would affect the gene pool of all captive tigers. We might also unknowingly breed her with a close relative. No, we must make every effort to make the genetic pool as diverse as possible. The survival of the world's tigers may depend on it."

4. What genetic criteria should you consider when selecting a mate for Jambi? Criteria should include some or all of these: Gene diversity, Founder Genome Equivalents (FGE), Mean Kinship Value, and Inbreeding Coefficient.

5. Which female tiger do you decide to mate with Jambi? Why? There are several decent matches, but the best mate would be Sasha. If two cubs were born, the net change in gene diversity for all of captive Sumatran tigers would increase by 0.10%, rising from 91.41 (that is, 91.41% of the original gene diversity still retained) to 91.51. And because Sasha comes from Dallas-a nearby, warm-weather zoo-she would suffer few ill effects from the transfer and probably breed successfully. While other females might produce a higher gene diversity, our management group agrees that Sasha is the best choice.

5. What happens when Jambi tries to mate with the female tiger you chose? Various results, depending on which female the student chose. Some females are not very fertile, others too old or too young. Several will bear cubs--in these cases, the difference comes from the effect on the genetic pool of captive tiger. Some choices will improve the genetic pool, others will not.

6. At the end of the game, did you get a successful breeding? Are cubs born? Some matings will result in cubs, others will not, but students always have a chance to keep trying until they get a successful mating.
