How do zoos feed and care for tigers?

In the wild, tigers kill and eat a prey animal once or twice a week. The rest of the time, they rest, or try to hunt for prey.

It's hard to recreate this situation in a zoo by putting a live prey animal into the exhibit. People would get very upset. Also, leaving dead animal bodies to rot in the exhibit would be dangerous to the tiger's health.

 


 A diet of relying entirely on commercial feline food can cause healthy teeth (left) to develop tooth and gum problems (right)

Many zoos feed animal carcasses to their tigers. Tigers which gnaw on caracasses, bone and all, have fewer gum and teeth problems. Carcasses can also make zoo tigers happier and more active. But they also may contain germs which could make the tiger sick.
Other zoos feed their tigers on a commercial feline food. Biologists and nutrionists designed this food to give the tiger all the nutrients it needs. Commercial food has no dangerous germs and lets the keeper keep track of how much food the tiger eats.
Most zoos feed their tigers on a mix of commercial food and animal carcasses.

No matter which diet is used, all zoos give their tigers a cow's kneebone (called a knucklebone) once a week to avoid dental problems.

 Where you feed the tiger is as important as when you feed the tiger. If you feed the tiger in the exhibit, you cannot examine it or monitor its diet. Sick tigers rarely show any symptoms except a poor appetite, so it is very important to watch carefully how much a tiger eats at night. After three days without eating, a medical exam is necessary.
