How much space does a zoo tiger need?

An important goal for zoos is to teach people about animals and the natural world. By giving visitors a good view of the tiger, zoos can help inspire them to learn and care about animals and nature. The zoo exhibit can't be too big, or people won't be able to see the tigers. And it can't be too small—that would be unhealthy for the tiger, and many visitors would become upset to see the tiger locked up in such a way.

A Home That's Just Right!

A good tiger exhibit is not too big and not too small. A zoo tiger needs at least 30 meters by 50 meters (100 feet by 150 feet). That's about the size of two house lots. A large exhibit would be about 80 meters by 150 meters (250 feet by 475 feet).

"Mommy, where's the Tiger?"

Visitors should be able to see the tiger when they come to the zoo. The closer the better!

Some zoos have large spaces but keep the tigers in a smaller area close to the visitors for good viewing. "Hot wires" (harmless electric fences) keep the tiger in view. An exhibit can seem larger and more natural without hiding the tigers from view.


Keeping Tigers and Humans Safe

Zoos have to keep their wildlife and visitors safe. Some zoos still use bars, but many zoos now try to build barriers of wire, glass, or water which do not block visitors' views of the animals. 
Wire fences should be strong and tall. They should be buried deep to keep tigers from digging their way out.  
  Glass looks nice and gives visitors a good, nose-to-nose view of the tiger. It costs more and is harder to tke care of. It also blocks the sound and smell of the animals.
Moats use water to keep tigers in and let visitors have a clear view of the tigers. Moats have to be deep and wide so the tiger can't get out.

Zoos in cold climates can't use moats because they would freeze and the tiger could walk away.

Night Rooms

A tiger exhibit must also have night rooms for the tiger to sleep in. Night rooms let zookeepers watch the tigers' health closely and keep the tigers safe at night.
A squeeze cage, shown here, is a simple way to give shots. As the tiger moves through the cage, it is briefly squeezed against the bars so the keeper can inject its shoulder with the medicine. The tiger is freed before it knows what has happened.
